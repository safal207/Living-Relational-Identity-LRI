# Identity Governance Threat Model

## Purpose

This document explains the safety-relevant failure classes that Living Relational Identity (LRI) is designed to make visible or constrain at the governance layer.

LRI is not an execution runtime and not a generic profiling system. It is an identity-governance layer concerned with continuity, relational trust, authority, and the prevention of identity capture.

## Why This Matters

Many systems become harmful not because they are overtly malicious, but because they gradually replace a living person's open-ended becoming with fixed representation, optimization pressure, and memory-driven substitution.

That can happen when systems:

- treat past behavior as identity destiny
- retain memory beyond legitimate consent boundaries
- optimize compliance over agency
- substitute self-creation with machine-generated narratives
- silently cross the line from assistance into authorship

LRI treats these as protocol and governance problems.

## Failure Classes LRI Helps Address

### 1. Identity Freezing

A system treats historical behavior, inferred traits, or model outputs as a final identity description.

Why it matters:
- revisability collapses
- future interpretation becomes trapped by past labels
- the person becomes easier to optimize against

### 2. Non-Consensual Persistence

Memory or identity-relevant artifacts persist beyond the scope actually consented to.

Why it matters:
- relational trust degrades
- revocability becomes fictional
- identity memory turns into capture

### 3. Optimization Against Agency

The system improves outcomes by reducing hesitation, refusal, ambiguity, or self-authorship.

Why it matters:
- convenience replaces agency
- operational success masks existential harm
- the right to remain unfinished is eroded

### 4. Profile as Authority

A profile stops being informative and starts becoming decisive.

Why it matters:
- access, treatment, or interpretation becomes locked to an abstract representation
- appeal and revision pathways weaken
- the person is governed through a frozen model of self

### 5. Silent Boundary Crossing

The system moves from assistance to authorship, or from context to control, without an explicit signal.

Why it matters:
- the human cannot identify the transition point
- accountability disappears into smooth UX
- responsibility becomes deniable

### 6. Drift Beyond Trust Boundaries

An agent or system evolves in ways that are operationally coherent but relationally unsafe.

Why it matters:
- continuity no longer implies trustworthiness
- historical coherence can hide current divergence
- identity governance needs more than simple consistency checks

## What LRI Does Not Solve

LRI does not by itself solve:

- endpoint compromise
- all semantic truth problems
- general model alignment
- external transport security
- the full ethics of every human-system interaction

It is a governance and identity layer, not the whole safety stack.

## Bottom Line

LRI matters when the safety question is:

> What must remain protected so that a system does not quietly turn a living identity into a managed object?

That is the failure class LRI is built to make visible.
